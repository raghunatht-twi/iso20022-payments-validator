Why It's Hard

Five structural challenges that explain why most migrations stall, overrun, or deliver less than promised.
Why It's Hard · Challenge 1 of 5

The Legacy Trap

  • Architectural mismatch at the core — Core banking systems built for fixed-length MT fields cannot natively parse hierarchical XML/MX structures. The data models are fundamentally different.
  • The translation layer shortcut — Most banks chose MT↔MX coexistence to meet the deadline. This deferred the real work and has now created a second, harder migration.
  • Two-thirds of banks acknowledge the cost of keeping legacy infrastructure, yet most remain entangled in it (EY / Fulcrum Digital).
  • Budget depletion — Teams that spent years on Phase 1 compliance now face Phase 2 (native migration) with smaller budgets and lower executive appetite for another payments overhaul.

Why It's Hard · Challenge 2 of 5

The Data Problem Nobody Budgeted For

  • ISO 20022 demands structured data banks don't hold — LEIs, fully structured postal addresses, purpose codes, BICs in correct format. Legacy customer databases store this as free-text blobs.
  • November 2026 structured address mandate (CBPR+) — Unstructured addresses will be rejected by the network. Banks must remediate customer and beneficiary address data field-by-field at scale.
  • Counterparty data is even harder — A bank cannot fix its correspondent's data. Mismatched LEIs and truncated addresses from upstream banks break STP downstream.
  • Discovered late, always — Data quality issues consistently surface during testing, causing timeline collapse. Not scoped as a first-class workstream in most programmes.
Nov 2026
CBPR+ structured address deadline — 17 months away, most banks have not started

What needs to be structured

  • Customer postal addresses → street, building, town, postcode, country
  • Beneficiary addresses (millions of records)
  • Legal Entity Identifiers (LEIs) — validated and current
  • Purpose codes for transaction categorisation
  • Financial institution BICs in correct 8/11-char format
This is the next cliff edge. Banks that resolved Phase 1 (message format) still face a major data engineering programme before November 2026.
Why It's Hard · Challenge 3 of 5

Testing: The Hidden Bottleneck

  • Testing scope is uniquely broad — End-to-end validation must cover internal systems, correspondent banks, clearing houses, and corporate clients simultaneously. No single team controls all endpoints.
  • Banks exhaust testing windows — Dedicated test environments compete with BAU releases. The most commonly cited cause of schedule slippage in bank migrations.
  • Each network has its own implementation guide — CBPR+, Fedwire, CHIPS, SEPA, CHAPS each publish separate field usage rules. A message valid for one network fails on another.
  • Counterparty readiness gaps undermine STP — Even a fully native sender cannot achieve straight-through processing if correspondent banks send truncated or unstructured data.
  • Manual test data creation — Generating valid, realistic XML messages at scale for all message variants (pass, fail, edge case) is time-consuming and error-prone when done by hand.

Why It's Hard · Challenge 5 of 5

The ROI Gap

  • Benefits are long-term and diffuse — ROI only materialises at scale and only after full native adoption. A translation shim is compliant but captures none of the value.
  • The payoff is real — but delayed — Banks that went native are seeing measurable gains. The problem is the investment peaked years before the return.
  • Financial crime compliance is the biggest lever — Costs exceeded $1 trillion globally in 2024. ISO 20022's structured data directly reduces false positives and investigation cost.
  • Most banks can't yet connect the investment to the return — KPIs are defined around compliance milestones, not business outcomes. The CFO case is underdeveloped.

What Good Looks Like

ISO-native isn't a message format — it's a data strategy. Here's the target state and what it unlocks.
What Good Looks Like

What ISO-Native Actually Means

Compliance means your messages are in MX format. ISO-native means structured data flows end-to-end, without translation at any internal hop.

Message Format

XML/MX natively produced and consumed — no MT↔MX translation shim at any point in the chain

Structured Data

Enriched, validated fields throughout — names, addresses, LEIs, purpose codes — not free-text blobs

Governed Data Models

Data quality rules enforced at source, not patched at the network boundary before transmission

Native Downstream

AML, fraud, liquidity, and regulatory reporting systems consuming MX data directly — no enrichment pipeline

Most banks that "completed" migration are compliant on the first dimension only. The other three are where the value — and the November 2026 mandate — sit.
